Don’t just dump the error. Give the context.

Every day in game dev channels, Discord servers, and forums, someone drops a massive, uncontextualized stack trace into a chat or says something like:

User123: Hey, I'm getting AttributeError: 'Surface' object has no attribute 'rect'. Anyone know how to fix this?

This is a counterproductive way to get help.

What you are actually doing is asking the community to play detective. You are forcing everyone to magically guess how you structured your game loops, how you initialized your sprites, or what your asset folder looks like.

There are a million reasons why Python can throw an AttributeError or a TypeError. Without context, nobody can help you without dragging you through a slow, exhausting interrogation first.

Why this slows your game dev down

  1. The error is the symptom, not the story: A stack trace tells us where the engine finally gave up and crashed. It doesn't tell us how your logic got into that broken state five frames earlier.
  2. It wastes time: Instead of getting a fix, you trigger a back-and-forth Q&A session. "Where did you define self.rect?" "Are you using pygame.sprite.Sprite?" "Can we see your main loop?"
  3. It alienates the experts: The developers who know pygame-ce inside and out will often scroll right past a raw error dump because they don't have the energy to hunt down your source code.

The Solution: Provide the "Big Three"

Instead of dropping a raw error and expecting a psychic response, format your help request with these three vital pieces of information:

  1. The Goal: What gameplay mechanic or feature were you trying to implement?
  2. The Minimal Code: A small, readable snippet of the relevant class or loop (not a screenshot of your entire IDE).
  3. The Symptom: The full error message and a quick note on exactly when it happens (e.g., "It crashes the moment my player collides with an enemy").

Look at the difference:

❌ Bad:

"My game keeps crashing with pygame.error: video system not initialized. Why?"

Without seeing how you structured your main file or where you called pygame.init(), no one can tell you why your subsystem isn't ready.

✅ Better:

"I am trying to split my game into multiple files. I moved my player class to player.py, but now when I try to instantiate it, I get pygame.error: video system not initialized.

Here is how I'm starting the game in main.py:"

{% highlight python %} import pygame from player import Player # I create the player here before the main setup player = Player() pygame.init() screen = pygame.display.set_mode((800, 600)) {% endhighlight %}

"It seems like it crashes on line 5. Any ideas?"

By providing the context, someone can immediately reply: "Ah, you are instantiating your Player (which likely loads an image or uses a pygame method) before you actually call pygame.init(). Move player = Player() to after your display setup." Result: Your bug is fixed in 2 minutes instead of 2 hours.

TL;DR: Help us help you.

The next time your game crashes, don't just copy-paste the red text. Tell us what you are building, show us the relevant code snippet, and then show us how it broke.
